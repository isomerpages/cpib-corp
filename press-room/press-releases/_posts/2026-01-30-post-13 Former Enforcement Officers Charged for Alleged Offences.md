<p>On 30 January 2026, 13 Certis officers who were doing enforcement work
at the material time of the offences, were charged in Court with various
alleged offences:</p>
<p>a) <strong>Muhammad Fitri Bin Jaffar (“Fitri”)</strong> (38-year-old male
Singaporean);
<br>b) <strong>Muhammad Syafiq Bin Tahar (“Syafiq”)</strong> (39-year-old male
Singaporean);
<br>c) <strong>Mohamad Aidil Bin Abu Bakar (“Aidil”) </strong>(47-year-old
male Singaporean);
<br>d) <strong>Muhamad Hazim Bin Mohamed Jalil (“Hazim”)</strong> (33-year-old
male Singaporean);
<br>e) <strong>Sufian Bin Mohamed (“Sufian”) </strong>(45-year-old male Singaporean);
<br>f) <strong>Muhammad Faddley Bin Abdul Wahid (“Faddley”) </strong>(38-year-old
male Singaporean);
<br>g) <strong>Chan Wei Ying, Olivia (“Chan”)</strong> (陈薇嫣, 39-year-old female
Singaporean);
<br>h) <strong>Mohamed Fiaros Bin Mohamed Zakaria (“Fiaros”)</strong> (43-year-old
male Singaporean);
<br>i) <strong>Muhammad Haziq Bin Khamzah (“Haziq”)</strong> (31-year-old male
Singaporean);
<br>j) <strong>Muhammad Khairul Izzul Shah Bin Abdul Karim (“Khairul”) </strong>(38-year-old
male Singaporean);
<br>k) <strong>Nurazmy Bin Yusoff (“Nurazmy”) </strong>(33-year-old male Singaporean);
<br>l) <strong>Reggie Choo Beng Kwang (“Choo”)</strong> (朱民光, 32-year-old male
Singaporean); and
<br>m) <strong>Tan Wee Keong (“Tan”) </strong>(陈维强, 38-year-old male Singaporean).</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Criminal Breach of Trust Offences</em></strong>
<br>2. Sometime between 2019 and 2022, on separate occasions, Fitri, Syafiq,
Aidil, Hazim, Faddley, Chan, Fiaros and Haziq allegedly misappropriated
properties that were entrusted to them in their capacity as Enforcement
Officers. These properties comprised cartons and loose packets of duty-unpaid
cigarettes, e-vaporisers, multiple cartons of e-vaporiser pods as well
as other prohibited tobacco products valued at about S$18,327 in total.
In addition, Fitri, Syafiq, Aidil, Hazim and Haziq allegedly conspired
with each other on separate occasions to misappropriate these properties.</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Receiving Stolen Property Offences</em></strong>
<br>3. Sometime between 2021 and 2022, on separate occasions, Fitri, Syafiq,
Aidil, Hazim, Sufian, Faddley, Fiaros, Haziq, Khairul and Nurazmy allegedly
received stolen properties from one another and other individuals. These
stolen properties comprised sticks, loose packets and cartons of duty-unpaid
cigarettes and e-vaporiser pods valued at about S$1,438 in total.</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Obstruction of Justice Offences</em></strong>
<br>4. Sometime in 2022, on separate occasions, Faddley, Fiaros, Choo and
Tan allegedly obstructed the course of justice in relation to the Corrupt
Practices Investigation Bureau’s (CPIB) investigation:</p>
<p>i) Faddley allegedly disposed of e-vaporisers, e-vaporiser pods and duty-unpaid
cigarettes into the rubbish chute of a Housing and Development Board (HDB)
unit before opening the door to CPIB officers;
<br>ii) Fiaros allegedly deleted a note in his phone containing records of
45 e-vaporiser pods that he had misappropriated during his course of work,
after learning from Sufian that he was with CPIB officers;
<br>iii) Tan allegedly revealed information about CPIB’s investigation to
Choo who subsequently disposed of at least one e-vaporiser and three packs
of e-vaporiser pods that he possessed after Tan advised him to do so; and
<br>iv) Choo allegedly deleted his WhatsApp conversation with Tan in relation
to CPIB’s investigation.</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Offences Under Tobacco (Control of Advertisements and Sale) Act</em></strong>
<br>5. Sometime in 2022, Faddley, Syafiq, Hazim and Aidil were found to be
in possession of e-vaporisers and related components when they were caught
by CPIB. These items, used for their personal consumption, were allegedly
part of case seizures related to their course of work.</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Offences Under Customs Act</em></strong>
<br>6. Fitri, Syafiq, Aidil and Faddley, in the course of their duties, were
required to conduct disposal of duty-unpaid cigarettes and e-vaporisers
from the HSA disposal bins located at various checkpoints. They had allegedly
taken the cigarettes, which did not comply with the standardised packaging<sup>1</sup> requirement,
intended for disposal and retained them for personal consumption. The cigarettes
were found in their respective residences.</p>

<p>8. Any person who is convicted of an offence under Section 409 of the <em>Penal Code</em> shall
be punished with imprisonment of up to 20 years, and shall also be liable
to fine. Any person who is convicted of an offence under Section 411 of
the <em>Penal Code</em> shall be sentenced to an imprisonment of up to five
years, or a fine, or to both. Any person who is convicted of an offence
under Section 204A of the <em>Penal Code </em>can be fined or sentenced
to imprisonment of up to seven years or to both.</p>
<p>9. Under Section 16(3) (a) of the <em>Tobacco (Control of Advertisements and Sale) Act</em>,
the possession, use or purchase of e-vaporisers carries a maximum fine
of S$2,000.</p>
<p>10. Under the <em>Customs Act </em>and the <em>Goods and Services Tax (GST) Act</em>,
buying, selling, conveying, delivering, storing, keeping, possessing or
dealing in duty-unpaid cigarettes are serious offences. Offenders can be
fined up to 40 times the amount of duty and GST evaded and/or jailed for
up to six years.</p>

kept confidential, except when the Court finds that the person has made
