<p>On 20 May 2026, <strong>Ng Teck Lee ("Ng") </strong>(黄德利, 59-year-old male
Singaporean), Chief Executive Officer of Citiraya Industries Ltd (“Citiraya”)1
at the material time of the offences faced <strong><u>38</u></strong> additional
charges in Court. Ng was earlier charged and remanded on 4 December 2024
for allegedly committing a criminal breach of trust (“CBT”) offence and
a corruption offence, following his arrest in Singapore after remaining
outside jurisdiction for almost two decades. Ng’s additional charges are
as follows:</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Corruption Offences</em></strong>
</p>
<p>2. Between March 2003 and April 2004, on multiple occasions, Ng allegedly
gave gratification totalling S$35,000 to three individuals, either as inducements
or rewards, for the following:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Forbearing to completely crush the AMD chips intended for delivery to
Citiraya;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Forbearing to ensure that Citiraya sampled and weighed the gold scrap
that originated from 3M Singapore Pte Ltd (“3M”); and</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Showing favour to Citiraya by helping it to secure a recycling contract
with Seagate Technology International (“Seagate”).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>3. For his alleged actions, Ng faces <strong><u>three</u></strong> charges
under Section 6(b) of the <em>Prevention of Corruption Act (Chapter 241, Rev Ed 1993)</em> (“<em>PCA</em>”),
of which three are amalgamated charges pursuant to Section 124(4) of the <em>Criminal Procedure Code 2010</em> (“<em>CPC</em>”).</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Corruption Offences Relating to Ng Teck Lee’s Abetment by Instigating one Ng Teck Boon</em></strong>
</p>
<p>4. Between November 2003 and December 2004, on multiple occasions, Ng
allegedly did abet by instigating his brother, Ng Teck Boon, to give gratification
totalling about S$1,822,000 and US$200,000 to seven individuals, either
as inducements or rewards, for similar reasons as listed in paragraph two
and including the following:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Forbearing to completely crush the AMD chips intended for delivery to
Citiraya;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ensuring that electronic waste of ST Microelectronics Asia Pte Ltd was
not completely crushed;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Expediting the delivery of chips that were not crushed completely from
AMD to Citiraya;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ensuring that scraps from Citiraya clients were not completely crushed;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Showing favour to Citiraya by recommending it as the sole service provider
to Seagate for the recycling of platinum shields; and</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Showing favour to Citiraya by enabling it to secure Seagate’s Asia and
Worldwide contracts for scrapping platinum shields, as well as extending
Citiraya’s yearly Singapore contract for the same purpose.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>5. For his alleged actions, Ng Teck Lee faces <strong><u>12</u></strong> charges
under Section 6(b) of the <em>PCA</em>, read with Section 109 of the <em>Penal Code (Chapter 224, Rev Ed 1985)</em> (“<em>PC</em>”).</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Criminal Breach of Trust Offences</em></strong>
</p>
<p>6. Between 2003 and 2004, on multiple occasions, Ng allegedly committed
CBT as a servant by dishonestly misappropriating about 89,760 kilograms
worth of electronic chips that were entrusted to him as the director of
Citiraya, and selling the said property in Hong Kong and Taiwan.</p>
<p>7. For his alleged actions, Ng faces <strong><u>one</u></strong> charge
under Section 408 of the <em>PC </em>which is amalgamated pursuant to Section
124(4) of the <em>CPC</em>.</p>
<p>8. In addition, Ng, who was entrusted with dominion over the electronic
scrap of Intel Resale Corporation and Agilent Technologies Singapore Pte
Ltd, weighing about 112,798 kilograms and 416 kilograms respectively, allegedly
committed CBT by dishonestly disposing these electronic scraps in violation
of the contract that Citiraya UK Ltd had with the two said companies. Ng
instructed certain employees of Citiraya not to crush these electronic
scraps, and to repackage and export them.</p>
<p>9. For his alleged actions, Ng faces <strong><u>two</u></strong> charges
under Section 406 of the <em>PC</em>.</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Theft Offence</em></strong>
</p>
<p>10. Between December 2004 and January 2005, Ng allegedly committed theft
of about 1,050 kilograms worth of electronic scrap from Citiraya's premises.
For his alleged actions, Ng faces <strong><u>one</u></strong> charge under
Section 381 of the <em>PC</em>.</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Cheating Offences</em></strong>
</p>
<p>11. Between November 2003 and January 2005, on multiple occasions, Ng
allegedly cheated a finance manager of 3M that its electronic scrap stated
in the refining statements yielded a lower content of precious metal when
in fact they were of a higher content.</p>
<p>12. In 2004, on multiple occasions, Ng also allegedly instigated the production
manager of Citiraya to cheat a senior accountant of AMD that its electronic
scrap yielded a lower content of precious metal when in fact they were
of a higher content.</p>
<p>13. For his alleged actions, Ng faces <strong><u>two</u></strong> charges
under Section 417 of the <em>PC</em>, of which one charge is read with Section
109 of the <em>PC</em>. Both are amalgamated charges pursuant to Section
124(4) of the <em>CPC</em>.</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Money Laundering Offences</em></strong>
</p>
<p>14. Sometime after April 2003, on two occasions, Ng allegedly transferred
a sum totalling approximately US$45 million, which represents his benefits
from criminal conduct, to two bank accounts held overseas. For his alleged
actions, Ng faces <strong><u>two</u></strong> charges under Section 47(1)(b)
punishable under Section 47(6) of the <em>Corruption, Drug Trafficking and Other Serious Crimes (Confiscation of Benefits) Act (Chapter 65A, Revised Edition 2000)</em> (“<em>CDSA</em>”).</p>
<p>15. On or about 30 June 2003, Ng allegedly instigated his wife, Thor Chwee
Hwa, to open an overseas bank account in her name to retain and conceal
the benefits from his criminal conduct. A sum of about US$1 million was
later transferred to the said account in July 2003. For his alleged actions,
Ng faces <strong><u>one</u></strong> charge under Section 44(1)(a) punishable
under Section 44(5) of the <em>CDSA </em>and read with Section 109 of the <em>PC</em>.</p>
<p><strong><em>Alleged Falsification of Accounts Offences</em></strong>
</p>
<p>16. Between June 2004 and January 2005, on multiple occasions, Ng allegedly
engaged in a conspiracy with several individuals to falsify 97 sales invoices
of Citiraya and six other companies. In relation to these 97 invoices,
Ng faces <strong><u>14</u></strong> charges under Section 477A of the <em>Penal Code</em>,
of which nine charges are read with Section 109 of the <em>Penal Code</em>.
12 charges are amalgamated pursuant to Section 124(4) of the <em>CPC</em>.</p>
<p>17. The maximum sentence faced by a person convicted on any of the above
charges is as follows:</p>
<ul data-tight="true" class="tight">
<li>
<p>Any person who is convicted of a corruption offence under Section 6 of
the <em>Prevention of Corruption Act</em> can be fined up to S$100,000 or
sentenced to imprisonment of up to five years or both;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Any person convicted of an offence under Section 216 of the <em>Penal Code </em>can
be fined or sentenced to imprisonment of up to 10 years or both;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Any person convicted of an offence under Section 381, 406 or 408 of the <em>Penal Code</em> can
be fined and sentenced to imprisonment of up to seven years;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Any person convicted of an offence under Section 417 of the <em>Penal Code</em> can
be fined and sentenced to imprisonment of up to three years. Whoever is
convicted of criminal breach of trust as a servant may be imprisoned for
up to 15 years, and shall also be liable to a fine;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Any person convicted of an offence punishable under Section 44(5) or 47(6)
the <em>CDSA (Chapter 65A, Revised Edition 2000)</em> can be fined up to
$200,000 or sentenced to imprisonment of up to seven years or to both;
and</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Any person convicted of an offence under Section 477A of the<em> Penal Code</em> can
be fined or sentenced to imprisonment of up to 7 years or both.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>18. Singapore adopts a strict zero-tolerance approach towards corruption
and takes a serious view of offences involving fraud or dishonesty, especially
when committed by individuals in positions of trust and authority. Such
conduct undermines corporate governance, erodes public confidence in the
integrity of businesses, and tarnishes Singapore’s reputation as a trusted
international business hub governed by the strong rule of law.</p>
